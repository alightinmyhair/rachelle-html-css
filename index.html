Facts about Rottweilers

Here are some quick facts about this breed of dog:

  1. Size:
    • 80-135lbs (depending on whether it is a male or female)
  2. Bred for::
    • Guarding and protection work
    • Since they were bred to work, Rottweilers do best with training and jobs to do, even if just as a child's companion.
  3. Exercise Requirements:
    • >40 minutes/day
    • Have a tendency to become obese if not excersized enough.
  4. Longetivity Range:
    • 8-11 years
  5. Social/Attention Needs:
    • Active, intelligent dogs
    • Rottweilers need extensive and continuous socialization to be good family companions
    • Aggression can be a problem, and this dog is fully capable of inflicting severe damage, so rottweilers do need a firm, patient hand and a knowledgeable owner.

Rottweilers are one of the oldest breeds and there is a town in Germany called Rottweil, named after the breed. They were a working dog breed that drove cattle to the market, pulled carts, guarded the homestead and even carried money in a money belt around their neck. Soon after my love of dogs started, I came across elephants in middle school. Something about elephants struck me. I cannot recall whether it was hearing how elephants were killed for their ivory but my love and passion for elephants grew.

Facts about Elephants

Here are some quick facts about elephants:

  1. Tusks:
    • These are actually long incisor teeth! These teeth continue growing throughout their lifetime.
  2. Trunk:
    • Elephants have one to two finger like projections (dependent on the species) making it easy to pick up small objects.
    • There are more than 100,000 muscles in a trunk!
  3. Intelligence:
    • Elephants have the largest brain of any land animal.
    • They mourn their dead and show empathy.

There are 3 surviving elephant species today:

  • Asian elephant
  • African savanna elephant
  • African forest elephant

These three species differ in anatomical and behavioral differences. But sadly, each species are in decline and of each species only a few hundred thousand remains. Thus, because they are "keystone species", which means they are integral to other species, if they become extinct this means entire ecosystems would change drastically.
